Integral equation of the first kind

Fudong Wang
Last updated on Feb 9, 2022 5 min read integral equation, complex analysis, mathematical physics

Background

Integral equations play important roles in mathematical physics. In this short note, we are going to discuss a class of integral equation of the first kind. My motivation to study those equations is from soliton gas, where the nonlinear dispersion relation is as follows: $$\int_\Gamma g(z,w)u(w)|dw|+\sigma(z)u(z)=\phi(z),\quad z\in \Gamma$$

where $\Gamma$ is some simple smooth rectifiable curve in $\mathbb{C}^+$ and kernel $g(z,w)=\log\left|\frac{z-\bar w}{z-w}\right|$ is so-called Green kernel (i.e. it is the Green function to the Direchlet problem on the upper half plane.). The case when $\sigma\equiv 0$ is referred to as soliton condensate. As one can immediately see, the equation becomes Fredholm equation of the first kind. It is well-known that integral equation of the first kind is (in general) ill-posed in the sense of Hadamard. Here we are going to consider a slightly different equation: $$\int_{\Gamma}\log|w-z|^{-1} u(w)|dw|=f(z),\quad z\in \Gamma.$$

And we will consider the uniqueness of the equation using classical potential theory. Then we will use a method introduced by Yan&Sloan to solve the equation explicitly.

Potential theory and uniqueness

Let’s first introduce some definitions from classical complex analysis. All the definitions and theorems can be found on Hille’s book.

Definition 1. Let $\mu$ be a probability measure supported in $\Gamma$ (i.e. $\mu(\Gamma)=1$.), the logarithmic potential is $$U^{\mu}(z)=-\int_\Gamma \log\left|z-w\right|d\mu(w),\quad z\in \mathbb{C}.$$

Definition 2. The energy is defined as $$I(\mu)=\int U^{\mu}(z) d\mu(z).$$

Definition 3. The capacity of a compact set $\Gamma$ is defined as $$C_\Gamma=e^{-V_\Gamma}=\exp{(-\inf_{\mu\in M(\Gamma)}I(\mu))},$$ where $V_\Gamma$ is so-called Robin constant.

Definition 4. A charge is a signed measure, which always has the decomposition as $\sigma=\sigma_+-\sigma_-$, where $\sigma_\pm$ are called positive and negative part of the charge. And we denote the space of charge with finite energy as $S(\Gamma)$.

Now let’s list some classical results from Hille’s book.

Lemma 1. Let $\Gamma$ be a compact set with positive capacity, ans $S\subset\Gamma$ has zero capacity. If $\mu\in M(\Gamma)$ and $I(\mu)<\infty$, then $\mu(S)=0$. The same statement if also true for charges (exercise).

Lemma 2. If $C_\Gamma>0$, then there is a unique measure $\mu_e$ such that $I(\mu_e)=\inf_{\mu\in M(\Gamma)}I(\mu)$.

Lemma 3. Let $\Gamma$ be a compact set with positive capacity. Then $U^{\mu_e}(z)\leq V_\Gamma,\quad z\in \mathbb{C}$ and $U^{\mu_e}(z)= V_\Gamma,\quad z\in \Gamma, q.e.$.

Lemma 4. Let $\sigma\in S(\Gamma)$ and $\sigma(\Gamma)=0$. Then $I(\sigma)\geq 0$ and “$=$” iff $\sigma\equiv 0$.

Based on the lemmas we mentioned above, we can easily prove the following theorem.

Theorem 1. Let $\Gamma$ be compact and $\sigma\in S(\Gamma)$ generates constant potential, i.e. $U^{\sigma}(z)=c,z\in \Gamma$, q.e., then $c=\sigma(\Gamma)V_\Gamma$ and $\sigma=\sigma(\Gamma)\mu_e$.

proof. Write \red{$\sigma=\sigma(\Gamma)\mu_e+\sigma_0$}, then $\sigma_0(\Gamma)=0$ and it belongs to $S(\Gamma)$. Thus for each $z\in \Gamma$, we have $$ U^{\sigma_0}(z)=c-\sigma(\Gamma)V_\Gamma, q.e. $$ Then $$ I(\sigma_0)=(c-\sigma(\Gamma)V_\Gamma)\sigma_0(\Gamma)=0. $$ By lemma 4, $\sigma_0\equiv 0$, and the result follows.

Corollary 1. Suppose $\Gamma$ is compact with $C_\Gamma\neq 1$, if $\sigma\in S(\Gamma)$ is such that $U^\sigma(z)=0,z\in \Gamma, q.e.$, then $\sigma\equiv 0$.

Theorem 2.(Uniqueness) Let $\Gamma$ be a piecewise smooth with finite length and $C_\Gamma\neq 1$, assume $g\in L_p(\Gamma)$ with $p>1$ such that \begin{align*} -\int_\Gamma \log|z-w| g(w)|dw|=0,\quad \forall z\in \Gamma, \end{align*} then $g\equiv 0$.

Solution Methods

We are now in the position to construct the solution. The method is due to Yan-Sloan, see reference[3] for more details. The existence will follow as we find a way to construct it. First, let’s recall some notations for Sobolev spaces.

Let $C^\infty_e$ denote the set of even smooth $2\pi$ periodic functions on $\mathbb{R}$. For each $v\in C_e^\infty$, we have \begin{align*} v(s)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\left(\sum_{m\in\mathbb Z_+}\hat v(m)\cos{ms}+\frac{1}{2}\hat v(0)\right), \end{align*} with $ \hat v(m)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\int_0^\pi v(s)\cos{(ms)}ds, m=0,1,2,\cdots. $ Define the weighted norm in terms of the Fourier series: \begin{align*} |v|^2_t=2\sum_{m\in \mathbb{Z}_+} m^{2t}|\hat v(m)|^2+|\hat v(0)|^2. \end{align*} Definition [Weighted Sobolev space] $$H_e^t(2\pi) = \text{Closure}\{C_e^\infty(2\pi),|\cdot|_t\}.$$

To connect it to our equation, we recall the following well-know indenty: For any $v\in H^0(2\pi)$, we have \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi\log\left|2e^{-1/2}\sin\frac{t-\tau}{2}\right|v(\tau)d\tau=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left(\sum_{|m|>0}\frac{\hat v(m)}{|m|}e^{imt}+\hat v(0)\right):=Av(t). \end{align*}

The operator $A$ defined above has very good properties. In fact, $A$ is an unitary operator from $H^t$ to $H^{t+1}$ for any $t\in \mathbb R$. Moreover, When $t>0$, we have \begin{align} A^{-1}v=-D^2Av+Jv=-DADv+Jv=-HDv+Jv,\quad v\in H^{t+1},\nonumber \end{align} where $Jv=(1/\sqrt{2\pi})\hat v(0)$, $Dv(t)=v'(t)$, and $H$ is the Hilbert transform: \begin{align*} Hv(t)=-\frac{1}{2\pi} p.v.\int_{-\pi} ^\pi \cot{\frac{t-\tau}{2}}v(\tau)d\tau. \end{align*}

Existence for simple smooth closed curves

Consider a closed smooth simple curve $\Gamma$. Then there is a function $v$ from $C^{\infty}(2\pi)$ such that $v: [-\pi, \pi]\rightarrow \Gamma$ and $v'\neq 0$. Then the integral equation of the first kind can be transferred to $$Kw(t):=-\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\log|v(t)-v(\tau)|w(\tau)d\tau=f(t),\quad t\in [-\pi, \pi],$$ where $w(\tau)=u(v(\tau))|v'(\tau)|$ and $f(t)=\phi(v(t))$

Theorem If $\Gamma$ is smooth closed and simple with $C_\Gamma\neq 1$, then $K:H^t\rightarrow H^{t+1}$ is bijective.

Proof. Write $K=A+B$, where the kernel of $B$ is $-\frac{1}{\pi}\log\left|e^{1/2}\frac{v(t)-v(\tau)}{2\sin(\frac{t-\tau}{2})}\right|$, which is smooth! This implies $B$ is compact. Then note $K=A(I+A^{-1}B)$, and that $A$ is unitary, apply standard Fredholm theory, the result follows.

An Examples from El-Tovbis: simple open arc case

Let $\Gamma=[-iq,iq],q>0$, the integral equation is \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{\pi}\int_\Gamma\log|z-w|u(w)|dw|=\Im z,z\in \Gamma. \end{align*} Apply Yan-Sloan’s method, set $v(x)=-iqx,a(t)=v(cos(t)),t\in [0,\pi].$ The equation becomes \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi\log|\cos(t)-\cos(\tau)|g(\tau)d\tau=f(t), \end{align*} with $g(t)=u(-iq\cos(t))\sin(t)$ and $f(t)=-\cos(t)+\frac{\log q}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\hat g(0)$.

The using one of the two identities, we have \begin{align*} \begin{cases} \hat q(0)\log (q/2)=0\ \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\hat g(1)=-1\ \hat g(m)=0,m>1. \end{cases} \end{align*} This implies $g(t)=-\cos t+c$, where $c=\begin{cases} 0,q\neq 2\\ \in i\mathbb R , q=2.\end{cases}$. Since when $q=2$, $C_\Gamma=1$! Then we have \begin{align*} u(z)= (-iz+ic)/\sqrt{q^2+z^2}. \end{align*} In El-Tovbis, they assume $u$ is anti-Schwarz sysmmetrix, then $c$ must be 0.
